Storefront

The First Impression

Route 66 Entrance

The moment you see it from the street, you know this isn't another Tel Aviv burger spot. A tilted neon sign hangs above the door, casting red light across the sidewalk. A vintage Route 66 shield marks the entrance. Inside the vestibule, a hand-painted mural of an American highway stretches into the desert. The glass door is framed in brushed steel with a brass pull handle. Before you've ordered, you're already in America.

Seating

The Booth Experience

Red Leather Booths

Eight seats of deep-tufted oxblood leather, each booth a private world separated by dark walnut partitions. Chrome edge trim catches the neon. Pendant lights in brushed brass hang low, creating pools of warm amber. The checkered tile floor grounds it in classic American diner DNA. Between the booths, framed black-and-white photos of Memphis, Chicago, and the Mississippi Delta line the brick wall.

The Bar

The Bourbon Bar

Ten Stools. One Bar.

Dark walnut counter polished to a mirror finish. Behind it, a full mirrored wall reflects rows of bourbon, rye, and single malts. Chrome beer taps stand at attention. The bartender works under a neon "BOURBON" sign whose red glow turns every glass into a jewel. Five craft beers on tap — two local, three imported. This is where you start the night, and where you end it.

Music

The Corner Stage

Where the Blues Live

A raised oak platform, a single spotlight overhead, warm tube amplifiers glowing amber on each side. A vintage Shure SM58 on a chrome stand. Room for a three-piece — guitar, bass, drums. Twenty seats face the stage in a gentle semicircle. Thursday nights, the house lights dim, the neon hums, and for two hours this corner of Old North Tel Aviv becomes the Mississippi Delta.

Décor

The Guitar Wall

Twelve Stories on Brick

Twelve instruments, each mounted on custom-forged iron brackets against exposed brick. A 1962 Gibson ES-335 sits beside a road-worn Fender Telecaster. Below them, a resonator guitar from the Delta. Between the instruments, small brass plaques tell each guitar's story. A single neon tube traces the word "BLUES" between the second and third row. It's museum, shrine, and conversation piece in one.
